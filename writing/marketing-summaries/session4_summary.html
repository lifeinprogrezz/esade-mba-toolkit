Session 4 Summary: Value Selling at SKF Service and the Education Challenge

MBA · Marketing Planning & Implementation

Note: I missed Sessions 3 and 4. Following the rule that students who missed two sessions submit a summary of the sessions they missed minus one, this document covers Session 4 only.

The question the session was really about

Session 4 picked up where Session 3 on Pearson left off. Session 3 dealt with how a company can build its strategy around the outcomes its customers actually achieve, not just the products they buy. Session 4 took the next step and asked a harder, more practical question. When you genuinely create more value than your competitors, how do you actually get paid for it?

The honest answer the lecturer kept coming back to is uncomfortable. Value is not self-evident, but price always is. A buyer can read your price tag in a second. Understanding why your bearing lasts longer, or why your service prevents costly downtime, takes effort that most buyers will not put in, especially when their boss is pushing them to cut costs. As one of the slides put it bluntly, there is always someone willing to do it cheaper. That is the trap SKF found itself in.

SKF: a great company drifting toward commoditization

SKF was the global leader in bearings, with about 20% world share, deep technical expertise, and a brand that genuinely stood for quality. None of that was enough on its own.

Three forces were pulling the company toward becoming just another commodity supplier. First, end users were consolidating, and their professional purchasing teams were gaining weight inside the customer organizations. The maintenance engineers who used to appreciate SKF's superiority were being pushed aside by buyers whose job was simply to extract lower prices. Second, distributors were turning into the purchasing arms of their customers, often demanding flat across-the-board discounts. The famous "five-off" in pulp and paper was the example used in the case, where a 5% across-the-board cut translated into roughly a 50% loss in supplier margin on some products. Third, low-cost competitors from Eastern Europe and China were entering with "good enough" alternatives, eroding SKF's pricing power from below.

The net result was that SKF's customers had stopped caring about most of the things that made SKF special. They just wanted a lower number on the invoice.

The response: DSP and value-based selling

SKF's answer to this pressure was the Documented Solutions Program. Instead of telling customers their bearings were better and hoping they would believe it, SKF started measuring it. The example in the case showed a customer investing $2.29 million in SKF bearings and related services, and getting back roughly $13.6 million in savings over 54 months. That is close to a 500% return, driven mostly by fewer pump failures, less labor, and lower spare-part consumption.

This is exactly the framework that the lecturer built up during the session. The chain runs from offering, to solution, to impact, to return. The customer first needs to understand what they are getting in their own context (the offering becomes a real solution). The seller then has to prove the impact in numbers the customer can verify. Finally, both sides need to share the upside, otherwise the customer has no reason to engage in the work involved.

The slides also showed a useful funnel for narrowing things down. Out of all the possible benefits a product could deliver, the seller first filters for the ones that are relevant to that specific customer, then for the ones that can actually be quantified, and then for the ones that can be verified after the sale. The MIT Sloan article we were given for this session calls this the ABC problem: going from product attributes, to customer benefits, to shared cash. The hardest part is not the math. It is forcing engineers to translate technical specs into customer dollars. Todd Snelgrove, the architect of DSP at SKF, kept asking colleagues "so what?" until they stopped saying "this bearing requires less lubrication" and started saying "this saves the customer a measurable amount of labor and material every year."

Pricing as a strategic lever, not just a number

The HBR reading by Bertini and Wathieu sat behind a lot of the discussion. Their argument is that price itself can be used to fight commoditization, not just suffer from it. They describe four moves. First, change the structure of pricing so it points at the value you deliver, like Goodyear pricing tires by mileage or Norwich Union charging per mile driven. Second, willfully overprice to make customers stop and think, which is what Burt's Bees and Apple do. The article actually mentions SKF directly as a company that holds a 30 to 40% premium and uses that premium to signal real superiority. Third, partition the price so customers notice features they would otherwise ignore. Fourth, equalize prices across options, so that customers stop hunting for the cheapest variant and start asking which option suits them best, like the original 99-cent iTunes pricing. The common thread is that pricing is not a finance question. It is a tool for shaping how customers think about your offer.

The Steelcorp dilemma

The case put SKF's strategy under real stress. Steelcorp, a $30 billion steelmaker buying around $12 million of bearings a year through ITC, decided that all future bearing purchases would go through a reverse auction. Lowest unit price wins. SKF was invited to participate, mostly because ITC, the distributor, urged them to.

Phil Knights had to choose. If he bid, SKF could probably win the order, but in doing so he would quietly admit that all the work on DSP and value selling could be set aside whenever a tough buyer pushed hard enough. If he refused, SKF risked losing about $4 million in annual sales and damaging its relationship with ITC, which handled most of its US business. Inside the company, Todd called reverse auctions "the ultimate commoditization tool" and wanted to walk away. Steve and ITC's Brad both pointed at the real revenue and the long partnership at stake, especially with the economy already turning down.

What I would recommend

I would not bid in the reverse auction, but I would also not just send a polite refusal and walk away.

Bidding sends a clear signal across the company and the market. When a buyer pushes hard, SKF folds. That undoes years of work. Other large customers would notice, and value selling would lose credibility internally as well. The sales force, who were trained to be "farmers" building relationships and quantifying impact, would have little reason to keep doing the hard work if the company itself does not back the strategy when it is under pressure.

But refusing without engaging would also be a mistake, because it would damage ITC and hand Steelcorp's business to Timken on a silver platter. The better move is to use this moment as a reason to do real value selling at the very top of Steelcorp. Phil should ask, with ITC's support, for a direct meeting with John Elliot, the new VP of purchasing, and bring DSP. The pitch is straightforward. A reverse auction optimizes one number, the unit price, and ignores total cost of ownership. In a steel mill that cost is dominated by downtime and maintenance, not by the sticker price of the bearing. SKF can show, in Steelcorp's own numbers, what cheaper bearings would actually cost over a year. If, after seeing the math, Elliot still insists on running the auction, then Steelcorp is signalling that it is not the right kind of customer for this strategy, and SKF can let Timken win that order with eyes open.

What I am taking away from the session

The biggest idea from Session 4 is that pricing is a strategy question, not a finance question. How you charge customers, and what you can prove to them, shapes who you can be as a company. Discounting away your differentiation feels like saving a deal in the moment, but every time you do it the next conversation gets harder. Customers learn very quickly which suppliers will hold their prices and which will not.

The second takeaway is that value-based selling only works if the whole company is set up for it. The session ended with a traffic-light view of the conditions. On the company side you need a culture that takes value seriously, enough bargaining power to avoid being cornered, and creative financing options. On the sales team side you need education, the right tools (like DSP), and compensation that rewards holding price rather than just hitting volume. Plenty of companies talk about value selling without doing any of this work, and that is why most of these initiatives fade after a year or two.

Finally, this session connected back to Pearson in a way I found useful. Real strategy is hard because it asks you to give something up. Pearson had to accept that some products would not pass their efficacy bar. SKF has to accept that some big orders are not worth chasing on the buyer's terms. The reward, if you can stick with it, is a business that is harder to copy and customers who come back because they actually got what they paid for.
