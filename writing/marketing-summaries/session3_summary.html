The question the session was really about

Session 3 set up the bigger arc that Session 4 then closed. The starting point was simple. Most companies sell things, a textbook, an insurance policy, a bearing, a piece of software. But customers don't actually want the thing, they want some outcome that the thing makes possible. A student wants to learn calculus, not own an app. A driver wants safe miles, not a tire. A factory wants production, not a ball bearing.

If that is true, why do almost all companies still charge for the thing instead of for the outcome? the lecturer's answer was that selling the product is the easy default. You hand over the product, get paid up front, and let the customer figure out the rest. The risk that the customer never opens the box, uses it badly, or doesn't get the result they wanted, all sits on the customer. That is comfortable for the seller, but it caps the size of the market and leaves real value on the table.

Session 3 was about what happens when a company tries to take on more of that risk in exchange for capturing more of the value.

From outputs to outcomes

The slides built up a useful contrast. A traditional business has inputs that produce outputs. A craftsman puts wood and effort in, a finished chair comes out, the customer pays for the chair, done. The outcome view is different. The chair only matters because it leads to access (someone can actually get and use it), consumption (someone uses it regularly), and performance (it is comfortable and lasts). Real value lives in those three things, not in the object itself.

The Bertini and Koenigsberg article ("Competing on Customer Outcomes") laid out three revenue models that follow this logic, each one a step further from "just sell the box":

1. Access models charge by time. Subscriptions, memberships, leases. HP Instant Ink, Spotify, Care by Volvo, Signify selling "light as a service" to airports. The seller takes on the small but real risk that the customer may never use the thing.

2. Consumption models charge by use. Michelin selling tires by the mile to truck fleets. Winterhalter charging per wash cycle for commercial dishwashers. Hello Tractor renting farm equipment by the job in Nigeria. Now the seller absorbs more risk, because they only get paid when the product is doing work.

3. Performance models charge by outcome. Orica selling not explosives but blast quality (the size of rock fragmentation), because that is what the mine actually cares about. Roche piloting personal reimbursement schemes for medication based on how the drug actually works for a given patient. The Teatreneu comedy theatre in Barcelona charging per laugh. Here the seller takes on most of the risk and gets paid only when value is genuinely delivered.

The pattern is that the further down this list you go, the more your incentives line up with the customer's. The trade-off is that you need much better data, sharper measurement, and a real foundation of trust.

Why this is harder than it looks

The session did not pretend this was an easy switch. The Casadesus-Masanell, Horváth and Velamuri reading made the point through case studies of companies that tried and failed.

MoviePass is the cautionary tale. They sold a $9.95 monthly subscription that let users see one movie a day in any cinema, and they paid full price for each ticket. The math simply did not work. The hope was that scale would unlock side revenues from data and advertising, but those side revenues never materialised at the scale needed to cover negative unit economics. Ofo, the bike sharing startup, ended the same way. Burning cash for growth only works if the growth eventually delivers profit per unit. If unit economics are broken, scale just makes the losses bigger.

The takeaway is that moving to outcome-based or subscription models does not fix a bad business. the lecturer's framing was honest. If you want to sell outcomes, you had better have done the homework on whether you can actually deliver them, and whether the price you can charge covers your real costs.

Pearson and the efficacy bet

The Pearson case put all of this into a real company. Pearson is the world's largest education company, with a huge catalogue of textbooks, courseware, assessments, and online schools. Under CEO John Fallon, the company committed publicly in 2013 to report on the actual learning outcomes of its products by 2018, alongside its financial statements. They called the strategy "efficacy."

The mechanics were ambitious. Pearson hired Sir Michael Barber to lead the work, built an internal efficacy framework, and started auditing its own products against it. They published 12 efficacy reports in 2018, peer-reviewed by SRI Education and audited by PwC. Some results were flattering, like MyLab and Mastering being linked to better exam performance. Others were uncomfortable, like Connections Academy virtual schools showing no statistical advantage over rival virtual schools on some measures. The point of efficacy was that Pearson would publish both kinds of results.

This was a real bet. Pearson was simultaneously transitioning from print to digital, divesting non-education assets like the Financial Times and Penguin Random House, and cutting headcount aggressively. Doing all of that while also rebuilding the company around a brand-new metric (learner outcomes) was a heavy lift.

My read on Pearson's situation

The work to date is genuinely impressive in scope, but the commercial payoff is still unproven. Three things stood out to me.

First, efficacy gave Pearson a language that is hard for cheaper, less rigorous competitors to match. When McGraw-Hill or Cengage start using "efficacy" in their own marketing without peer review or external audit, Pearson can credibly say theirs is the real thing. That is a genuine moat, but only if customers (mostly educators and institutions) actually care enough to make decisions on it. The case suggested some do, but many do not.

Second, the harder problem is the consumer side. The Aida Calculus app, priced at $2.99 a month, is a direct-to-learner product. Building a consumer brand around "Pearson means learning that actually works" would be powerful, but it takes years and serious marketing investment. The case quoted Tim Bozik admitting "there are no silver bullets in education." That is right, and Pearson does not yet have a consumer brand at the level of Spotify or Netflix.

Third, on pricing I think Pearson is being too cautious. Most of its products are still priced transactionally, while the underlying philosophy says they should be priced on outcomes. The MyLab and Mastering data is strong enough that I would push harder on outcome-linked pricing for at least one major product line, even at the cost of some short-term revenue. If you do not price on outcomes, you do not really live the strategy. Aida Calculus pricing as a subscription is a small but encouraging step.

What I am taking away from the session

The big idea is that the question "what do we sell?" is more interesting than it looks. Companies usually answer it with a product description. The better answer is some specific outcome the customer is paying for, and once you have that, almost everything downstream changes: your pricing model, your data needs, your sales pitch, your relationship with the customer.

The second takeaway is that you cannot copy this strategy. Outcome-based commercial models depend on having the right product (one that actually delivers measurable value), the right data infrastructure (so you can prove it), and the right culture (so the company is willing to share risk with the customer). Pearson is partway there. So is SKF, which we will see in Session 4. Most companies are nowhere near.

The third takeaway, which connects to the unit economics reading, is that all of this only works if the math works. A subscription that loses money on every customer does not become a good business by adding more customers. Before you redesign your revenue model around outcomes, you need to be sure you can actually deliver the outcome at a cost lower than the price someone will pay for it. Several real companies have failed because they skipped that step.
