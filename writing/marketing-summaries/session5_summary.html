Session 5 Summary: The Specification Challenge and Measuring Willingness to Pay

MBA · Marketing Planning & Implementation

The question the session was really about

Sessions 3 and 4 argued that companies should sell outcomes, not just products, and that pricing is a strategy lever rather than a finance number. Session 5 asked the obvious next question. If value is what we are really trying to deliver, how do we know what value our customers actually want, and how much they are willing to pay for it? the lecturer framed this as the specification challenge: the gap between what a customer wants and what we can get them to take.

His warning was blunt. The natural temptation in any company is to create first and capture later. Engineering builds something they like, marketing dresses it up, and only at the end does someone ask whether customers actually want it at the price required to make money. He listed the usual reasons this happens: arrogance, implied logic, an internal sense of purpose mistaken for a strategy, and plain caution. Juicero was the cautionary picture on the slide. A beautifully engineered $400 machine that pressed juice packets you could squeeze by hand. GoPro was the better counterexample, because it did not start from "we have a camera" but from "people want to share what they live."

The Customer Value Journey: Want / Get / See / Offer / Pay

The spine of the session was a five-step funnel that traces how value travels from the customer's head into the company's bank account. The customer starts with a want, which is some problem or aspiration. They eventually get something we have built. They see what we are charging and what they are getting in return. They offer a price they are willing to pay. They actually pay. Each gap between two steps is a different kind of marketing problem, and the lecturer mapped them as: specification challenge (Want to Get), education challenge (Get to See), conversion challenge (See to Offer), and customization challenge (Offer to Pay).

The point of putting them on one slide was to show that you cannot solve them in isolation. A perfectly specified product still fails if customers do not understand it. A great product that customers understand still fails if the price they offer is below what we need. Most companies treat these as silos owned by different functions. The session argued they are one continuous problem that the marketer has to own end-to-end.

Understanding problems, not designing products

The slide that hit hardest was the simple line "outputs are not outcomes." Companies are organized to produce outputs because outputs are easy to measure. The customer, however, is buying an outcome. the lecturer's example of two cemeteries in Santiago de Chile, one a manicured park for high-income families and one a chaotic public site decorated with umbrellas and flags, made the point. The output is the same in both: a place to bury the dead. The outcome wanted, and therefore the willingness to pay, is completely different. If you start from your output, you miss the real customer.

This connects back to Session 4. The reason SKF struggled was that it kept selling bearings (the output) when its industrial customers were buying uptime (the outcome). DSP worked because it spoke in the customer's language of dollars saved, not in SKF's language of revolutions per minute and lubrication intervals. The specification challenge is, in practice, the discipline of refusing to describe what you make, and forcing yourself to describe what changes for the customer because you exist.

Willingness to pay is the right measurement, and it is hard

Once you accept that you are selling outcomes, you have to measure their worth. the lecturer was very direct that liking, importance scores, and Net Promoter answers are not enough. Money speaks louder than words. The only number that ultimately matters is what the customer is willing to pay. The slide listed four critical decisions in measuring this well: revealed versus stated preference, direct versus indirect questions, causation versus correlation, and effort versus accuracy. Each is a real trade-off. Revealed data from real transactions is the gold standard but you only have it for the past and only at prices you have already tried. Stated data from surveys can explore new prices and new products, but people lie to themselves about what they would pay.

The Simon-Kucher pricing toolbox and the Calm case

Hans Munz from Simon-Kucher came in with the practitioner side of this. His first slide made the basic point: there is always an optimal price, and not knowing your elasticity costs you real money. The Audi Q7 launched too cheap, sold out, and left around 400 million euros on the table. The first Surface RT launched too expensive without ever testing customer perception, and Microsoft wrote down 900 million dollars of unsold inventory. Both errors come from skipping willingness-to-pay research.

He then walked us through four methods, two direct and two indirect. Van Westendorp asks customers four price questions (too expensive, expensive but acceptable, fair, too cheap) and finds where these curves cross. It is good for psychological thresholds but tends to underestimate optimal price. Gabor-Granger shows specific price points and asks for purchase likelihood at each, producing a price-volume curve. It is anchored, which is both a strength (you get an actual demand curve) and a weakness (the prices you show influence the answers). The Matrix of Competitive Advantages combines a MaxDiff importance score with a performance rating against competitors, telling you where you have real pricing power and where you are paying for features no one cares about. Conjoint analysis is the gold standard. Respondents make trade-offs between full product alternatives, and you back out the utility of each attribute, including price. From those utilities you can simulate market share, revenue, and profit at any price configuration.

Our class survey on the Calm meditation app, with 158 respondents, was the live example. Across all four methods the optimal price converged around 5.99 euros per month, well above Calm's actual Spanish price of 4.17 euros. Diversity of content and price came out as the two strongest value drivers in both MaxDiff and conjoint. Conjoint also showed that of the simulated Calm portfolio, the mid-tier "Standard" plan at 5.99 euros captured 40% of preference, while the cheap "Basic" plan only captured 27%. The methodological lesson is that none of the four methods is sufficient on its own. Used together, they triangulate a price range, confirm value drivers, and let you simulate decisions before going to market.

The Bertini, Aparicio and Aydinli reading: friction is not always bad

The MIT Sloan article we read for the session pushed against the e-commerce orthodoxy that all friction kills conversion. The authors argued that some friction in the purchase journey is not just acceptable, it is profitable. They identified three benefits. First, friction selects for better customers, the way Ferrari makes special editions hard to buy and luxury fashion brands hold queues. Second, friction enables better decisions, which reduces returns (a 15 to 30 cent drag per dollar of returned merchandise in many sectors) and builds the emotional ownership that Ikea has turned into a moat through self-assembly. Third, friction creates space to communicate, whether that is a rounding-up donation prompt, an unconventional price expression, or REI closing on Black Friday to make a point about consumerism.

The link to the rest of the session is direct. If you are competing on outcomes and willingness to pay, and your differentiation lives in things customers need a moment to appreciate, then a frictionless one-click purchase quietly destroys your value story. The article's "friction sweet spot" framework asks five questions: have you defined positive versus negative friction, what is the customer's involvement in the decision, what is your commercial objective, what is the customer's relationship stage, and which channel are you in. The answer is rarely "as little friction as possible."

What I am taking away from the session

The dominant idea is the contrast the lecturer drew at the end between "inside-out" and "outside-in" companies. Inside-out asks "what can we make, and what does it cost us, so what can we charge?" Outside-in asks "what can we fix for the customer, what is that worth to them, and how do we share that worth?" Almost every framework from the course so far (the Pearson efficacy story, SKF's DSP, Bertini and Wathieu on pricing as strategy, the Want / Get / See / Offer / Pay funnel) is some version of the same instruction to flip the company outward.

The second takeaway is that willingness-to-pay research is not optional once you take this seriously. If you are going to be paid for outcomes, you need to know how much those outcomes are worth to whom. The Simon-Kucher session made it clear that there are reliable methods for this, and that conjoint analysis in particular has a strong track record (the predicted versus actual volume change slide showed 16% versus 14% on one project, 5.0% versus 4.5% on another). The reasons companies skip it (cost, fear of the answer, "we know our customers") are usually the same reasons they end up with another Surface RT.

The third takeaway, from the friction reading, is that customer experience is not a synonym for ease. Sometimes the right experience is one that makes the customer pause, think, and own their decision. Removing friction at the moment of truth feels like progress for the conversion funnel, but it can quietly hollow out the brand and inflate returns. Done deliberately, friction is a marketing tool. Done by accident, the absence of friction is a way of giving away the value the rest of the company worked hard to build.
